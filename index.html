The situation: A colleague sends you a voice note describing a patient's condition, naming the village and the patient's occupation. Later that day, you see a photograph of a patient's wound posted in an 80-member WhatsApp group. The caption reads: "Interesting diabetic foot case from today's posting — can anyone identify the stage?"

The patient's face is partially visible. The neighbourhood is small.

Stage 1 · Question 1 of 3
A colleague sends you a voice note describing a patient's condition, naming the village and the patient's occupation. Which principle is most directly violated?
Not quite. Even without a name, details like village and occupation can re-identify someone in a small community. The duty of confidentiality applies to all identifiable information — not just names. Try again.
Correct. Patient confidentiality applies to all information that could reasonably identify a person — not only their name. The combination of location and occupation is often sufficient for re-identification.
Stage 1 · Question 2 of 3
Dr. Riya discovers the WhatsApp post three hours after it was shared. Her best first action is:
Not quite. Immediate deletion limits harm — but documentation and supervisor notification are also required for institutional accountability. Acting alone without a record leaves no basis for follow-up or learning.
Correct. Contain the breach first, then document and escalate. This sequence protects the patient and creates an institutional record. Silence after a breach is itself an ethical failure.
Stage 1 · Question 3 of 3
Which of the following is NOT a valid justification for photographing a patient for teaching purposes?
Not quite. Options A, B, and D are all legitimate pathways. Think about which option relies on intention rather than actual consent or de-identification.
Correct. Intent does not substitute for consent. "Just for learning" is not a legal or ethical justification — once shared digitally, the original sender loses all control over where the image goes.

Stage 2 of 3 · The Decision Maze

Stage 2
The Decision Maze

Two linked decisions. Choose the wrong path and you loop back. Choose wisely to advance.

New situation: You are a faculty member. A PG resident asks you to help write a case report about a patient they treated last month. They have detailed clinical notes but no documented patient consent. The patient has been discharged and is difficult to contact.

The resident says: "The case is genuinely rare — it will really help other doctors."

Stage 2 · Decision Point A
What do you advise the resident?
Wrong path. This leads to potential harm — and possibly a journal retraction. Educational benefit does not override the consent requirement, and casually changing details is not the same as ethics-board-reviewed anonymisation. Return to the decision point.
Correct path chosen. This balances educational value with ethical duty. Full anonymisation plus ethics board review is the accepted pathway when direct consent cannot be obtained. The maze continues — one more decision to go.
Stage 2 · Decision Point B
Update: The ethics board approves the case report with full anonymisation. But the resident insists on keeping the patient's exact age (67), city (Faridkot), and occupation (retired teacher) because "these are clinically relevant."
How do you respond?
Wrong path. The combination of age + city + occupation is often sufficient for re-identification in a small community, even without a name. Ethics board approval is for the process — not a blank cheque for any detail. Think about the minimum necessary information principle.
Excellent navigation. The minimum necessary information principle applies here. Age ranges, regional descriptors, and general occupational categories preserve clinical learning while protecting identity. You have cleared the maze.
